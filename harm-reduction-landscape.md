# Harm Reduction — Landscape Brief

*Prepared February 21, 2026 — for meeting with Isabella*

## What Harm Reduction Is

A public health framework that prioritizes reducing the negative consequences of drug use rather than requiring abstinence as a precondition for support. Rooted in pragmatism, dignity, and evidence.

**Core principle:** Meet people where they are.

## The Current Crisis (Michigan / National)

- **Fentanyl** has transformed the overdose landscape. Synthetic opioids now drive the majority of overdose deaths. In Michigan, opioid-involved deaths exceeded 2,900/year by 2022 and haven't meaningfully declined.
- **Polysubstance use** is increasingly common — fentanyl contaminating stimulant supplies (cocaine, meth), meaning even non-opioid users face overdose risk.
- **Xylazine ("tranq")** — veterinary sedative appearing in fentanyl supply. Creates severe wounds, doesn't respond to naloxone. Major emerging challenge for harm reduction workers.
- **Washtenaw County** (Ann Arbor / Ypsilanti) has active harm reduction infrastructure, but gaps remain — especially for rural-adjacent populations and the unhoused.

## Core Harm Reduction Services

| Service | What It Does | Data Generated |
|---------|-------------|----------------|
| Syringe Services Programs (SSPs) | Clean needle exchange, safer use supplies | Encounters, supply distribution volumes, referral pathways |
| Naloxone Distribution | Narcan kits to users, bystanders, first responders | Reversals reported, kit distribution, training completions |
| Drug Checking | Fentanyl test strips, spectrometry | Substance composition data, contamination alerts |
| MAT / MOUD | Medication for opioid use disorder (buprenorphine, methadone, naltrexone) | Treatment retention, dosing, outcomes |
| Wound Care | Mobile wound care for injection-related infections and xylazine injuries | Wound staging, healing trajectories, referral-to-care rates |
| Outreach | Street-based, encampment, shelter | Contact logs, needs assessments, warm handoffs |

## Where Technology Meets Harm Reduction

These are real gaps where someone with Ryan's skill set could make a meaningful difference:

### 1. Data Infrastructure
Harm reduction programs often run on spreadsheets, paper logs, or clunky legacy systems (e.g., ETO, HMIS). Research data collection is frequently disconnected from service delivery data. There's an opportunity for:
- Lightweight data collection tools (mobile-first, field-ready)
- Dashboards that make program data legible to funders and policymakers
- Integration between service data and research datasets

### 2. Research Tools
- Literature review management and synthesis
- Qualitative coding assistance (interview transcripts, field notes)
- Survey design and deployment
- Data visualization for publications and presentations

### 3. Community-Facing Tools
- Resource locators (where to get Narcan, SSP hours, MAT providers)
- Anonymous drug checking result reporting
- Peer support network tools

### 4. The Clay Connection
Art therapy and creative practice in addiction recovery is an established (if underfunded) field. Ceramics specifically:
- Tactile, embodied, grounding — the opposite of dissociation
- Process-oriented — you can't rush clay
- Community-forming — shared studio space creates structure and belonging
- Evidence base exists but is thin on ceramics specifically (more on art therapy broadly)

If her PhD research touches the intersection of creative practice and harm reduction, that's a genuinely underexplored area with publication potential.
