Project Overview

Isabella is a potter — serious work, technically strong — and a harm reduction practitioner with deep frontline experience. She has been accepted into a PhD program at the University of Michigan, focusing on harm reduction, public health, social work, and research. This project documents everything Ryan brings to support her PhD journey: software engineering, data systems, ceramics knowledge, AI tooling, and research infrastructure.

Core Principle

Meet people where they are. This is the organizing philosophy of harm reduction — and it guides this collaboration too. Don't build for the lab version of her research. Build for the real conditions: field encounters, uncertain connectivity, broken trust, limited time, and the weight of knowing the stakes.

Former Studio Member

Potter at Ryan's Ann Arbor studio. Clay is her outlet, her counterweight to frontline harm reduction work. Not a hobby — serious practice.

What She Does

Harm Reduction Practitioner

Works in addiction specialty and harm reduction. Has been in the field. Knows what fentanyl smells like when you test it. Knows the names.

Where She's Going

Just accepted. Program area: harm reduction, public health, social work, psychology, research. Timeline: 5–6 years. First meeting: February 22, 2026.

Harm Reduction Landscape

Harm reduction is a public health framework that prioritizes reducing the negative consequences of drug use rather than requiring abstinence as a precondition for support. Rooted in pragmatism, dignity, and evidence.

The Current Crisis

Fentanyl has transformed the overdose landscape. Synthetic opioids now drive the majority of overdose deaths. In Michigan, opioid-involved deaths exceeded 2,900/year by 2022. Polysubstance use is increasingly common — fentanyl contaminating stimulant supplies, meaning even non-opioid users face overdose risk. Xylazine ("tranq") is an emerging veterinary sedative appearing in the supply that creates severe wounds and doesn't respond to naloxone.

Where Technology Can Help

Gap 1

Data Infrastructure

Programs run on spreadsheets, paper logs, or clunky legacy systems. Research data is disconnected from service data. Opportunity for lightweight, mobile-first tools.

Gap 2

Research Tools

Literature synthesis, qualitative coding assistance, survey deployment, and data visualization for publications.

Gap 3

Community-Facing

Resource locators, anonymous drug checking reporting, peer support network tools.

Gap 4

The Clay Connection

Art therapy and creative practice in addiction recovery is established but underfunded. Ceramics specifically: tactile, process-oriented, community-forming. Thin evidence base — publication potential.

The Emotional Architecture

What nobody tells you about doing a PhD in a field where people die.

She has watched people overdose. She has called 911 and waited, counting breaths that weren't coming. She has handed a naloxone kit to someone who was shaking because their friend just died last week. And then she goes home and throws clay. Because the wheel doesn't judge, doesn't ask for progress reports, doesn't need a literature review. — from 01-emotional-architecture.md

Year by Year

Year 1 — The Translation Problem

Coursework & Literature Review

She walks into a classroom full of people who've read about harm reduction. She's done it. Imposter syndrome, but inverted: not "I don't belong here" but "does here deserve what I know?" Learning to write in passive voice about things she's seen with her own eyes.

Year 2 — The Narrowing

Qualifying Exams & Dissertation Proposal

She knows 47 things worth studying. She can only pick one. Committee dynamics are political. The qualifying exam is a hazing ritual disguised as academic assessment. She may have to choose between a topic she cares about and a topic her committee will support.

Year 3 — The Wall

Proposal, IRB, & Instrument Design

The proposal defense is the most vulnerable academic moment. IRB feels like the enemy (it's not — it protects participants — but when your protocol gets kicked back for the third time with "please clarify section 4.2.1," it feels adversarial). Most PhD students hit The Wall this year.

Year 4 — The Real Work

Data Collection in the Field

Participants will tell her things that change her. Someone in her study will overdose. Maybe die. She'll have to write it up as an "adverse event report." Secondary traumatic stress is real. The software needs to work perfectly.

Years 5–6 — Analysis & Writing

The Loneliest Period

The data will say things she doesn't want it to say. The tension between what the data shows and what the academy wants to hear. The final defense is both terrifying and anticlimactic. Post-defense depression is common. Remind her about pottery.

Specific Emotional Hazards

Moral Injury

She'll sit in classes where professors debate whether harm reduction "enables" drug use. She'll have to cite papers arguing against the thing she does every day. She'll have to engage charitably with positions that violate her values — while having held people who were turned away from abstinence-only programs.

The Name Problem

In the field, people have names. In the dissertation, they become Participant 7. This is ethically necessary and emotionally violent. Every time she writes "Participant 7 reported..." she knows exactly who that is.

The Identity Split

Field workers: "You left us. You're studying us now." Academics: "You're too close to the population. That's bias, not expertise." Neither is true. Both will sting.

What a Good Collaborator Looks Like

Availability without hovering. Competence without ego. Honesty about timelines. Understanding of the stakes. Respect for the subject matter. And space for clay — sometimes she needs to not think about the PhD. If you're working on software together and she says "I need to go throw," the correct response is "go."

03

Research Infrastructure

Most research software is built for labs. Harm reduction research is the opposite of that. Participants may not have a phone that works next week. They may give a different name each time. They might die between data collection points. The software has to be built for that reality.

The Research Lifecycle

Phase 1 — Years 1–2

Literature & Planning

Study extraction database: citation, population, methods, key findings. Auto-tagging by theme. Gap identification. This alone saves 100+ hours over the PhD.

Phase 2 — Year 2–3

IRB & Instruments

Survey builder with skip logic, consent workflow, participant enrollment with self-generated unique codes, instrument version control.

Phase 3 — Years 3–4

Field Data Collection

Offline-capable PWA, encrypted sync, interview recording via Whisper (local — no cloud), real-time data quality monitoring, researcher safety check-in system.

Phase 4 — Years 4–5

Analysis & Reporting

Qualitative coding interface, AI-assisted initial coding (local LLM), inter-rater reliability tracking, export pipeline for R/SPSS/Stata, automated IRB reports.

The Unique Identifier Problem

This is the hardest technical problem. You need to track the same person over time to measure outcomes. But you can't use real names (legal risk), SSNs (many don't have them), phone numbers (they change), addresses (many are unhoused), or biometrics (trust-destroying).

Solution Approach

A participant enrollment/matching system using multi-factor self-generated codes (configurable questions), fuzzy matching on partial code + demographics, hash-only storage (never the real name), duplicate flagging for human review, and de-identified longitudinal dataset export.

04

Validated Instruments

She won't build survey instruments from scratch. Her committee and IRB expect established measures. The challenge: most were validated in clinical settings. Harm reduction happens in parking lots, under bridges, in needle exchange vans.

Tier 1 — Substance Use Assessment

ASSIST — Alcohol, Smoking & Substance Involvement Screening Test
Free / WHO 5–15 min 20+ languages

Assesses risk level across 10 substance categories. Skip logic: if "no" to lifetime use, skip remaining questions for that substance. Scoring produces Low/Moderate/High risk levels. The software must handle the injection use flag (Q8) which triggers immediate high-risk classification regardless of total score.

DAST-10 — Drug Abuse Screening Test
Free / Public Domain 1–2 min

10 yes/no questions. Classic data quality trap: Q3 is reverse-scored — software must handle the reversal automatically. If DAST-10 ≥ 6, flag for full ASI administration.

TLFB — Timeline Followback
15–45 min Requires training

Calendar-based recall method. This is where the software adds enormous value: interactive calendar widget, auto-populate anchor dates (holidays, paydays), "I have no idea" option, drag-to-fill for patterns, auto-calculate all derived measures (percent days abstinent, longest streak). REDCap cannot do this well.

Tier 2 — Mental Health & Wellbeing

⚠ Critical Safety Requirement: PHQ-9 Q9 (suicidal ideation) MUST trigger an immediate safety protocol if the response is anything other than 0. This means visual alert, display of protocol steps, pause of survey, and logging that the protocol was activated. Non-negotiable from an IRB and ethical standpoint.
PHQ-9, GAD-7, PCL-5
Free / Public Domain 3–8 min each

Depression, anxiety, and PTSD severity. Often administered as a battery. PTSD prevalence in substance-using populations is 30–60%. PCL-5 cluster scoring maps to DSM-5 criteria. Common to administer PHQ-9 + GAD-7 together (~16 items, 5 minutes).

The Battery Problem

A "comprehensive" battery could run 56 minutes. Nobody in a parking lot during needle exchange gives 56 minutes. The art is cutting: what's required, what can be shortened (AUDIT-C instead of full AUDIT), what can be staggered across timepoints?

Practical Flows

Flow 1

Quick Field Screen — 10 min

DAST-10, AUDIT-C, K6, PHQ-9 Q9 only, 3 overdose items, 3 PRAPARE housing items. Auto-score, flag safety concerns, generate referral list.

Flow 2

Enrolled Baseline — 30 min

ASSIST, PHQ-9 + GAD-7, PCL-5, PRAPARE full, HRBS if injection involved.

Flow 3

Follow-Up Visit — 20 min

TLFB since last visit, PHQ-9 + GAD-7, service utilization, changed risk items. Auto-calculate change scores, flag clinically significant changes.

Ethical Complexities

Harm reduction research breaks the standard ethical framework. Participants are involved in illegal activity. They may be impaired during the research encounter. They may die during the study. The researcher may witness crimes, overdoses, and violence. And yet: this research is essential.

The Certificate of Confidentiality

A federal protection that prevents identifiable research data from being used in any legal proceeding — subpoenas, court orders, law enforcement requests. All NIH-funded research automatically receives one. But it has exceptions: mandatory child abuse reporting, duty to warn for imminent violence, and communicable disease reporting still apply.

Critical Protocols

Mandatory Reporting (Michigan MCL 722.623)

If Isabella is a licensed social worker, she is a mandatory reporter. Harm reduction complication: many participants are parents who use drugs. Discussing substance use patterns may indirectly disclose potential neglect — creating a chilling effect on disclosure that can kill the research. Solution: consent form clearly states the specific threshold; staff training on "reasonable cause to suspect" vs. any mention of parenting.

Suicidal Ideation During Research

Substance use and suicidality are deeply intertwined. The safety protocol (C-SSRS screening, risk determination, crisis resources) must be integrated into the software as an immediate, always-accessible protocol — never automated, always human-guided. PHQ-9 Q9 > 0 pauses the instrument instantly.

Overdose During a Research Encounter

This will happen. One-touch emergency protocol access, adverse event logging, automatic IRB notification workflow. Michigan's Good Samaritan Law provides limited immunity for calling 911 during an overdose.

Participant Death

In longitudinal research with this population, some participants will die during the study. The protocol must address how death is confirmed, how data is handled, IRB reporting (serious adverse event within 5 business days), and — critically — the impact on the researcher. A participant's voice is on recordings she's transcribing. A colleague, not a data point.

AI in Vulnerable Population Research

Using local LLMs for coding assistance requires consent disclosure: "Your de-identified transcript may be processed by AI software running on a secure, private computer (not sent to any cloud service) to assist with initial coding. A human researcher will review all AI-generated codes." Whisper transcription errors in sensitive material can change meaning — all transcripts require human review against audio.

The Seven Ethical Principles

  • Protect identity. No names, no GPS, no exact timestamps, no linkable data.

  • Expect the worst. People will overdose, die, disclose violence, attempt suicide. The system must handle all of these gracefully.

  • Consent is ongoing. Not a signature on a page. A continuous, informed, voluntary choice re-confirmed at each encounter.

  • Know the exceptions. The Certificate of Confidentiality is powerful but not absolute. Mandatory reporting, duty to warn, and medical emergencies override it.

  • Compensate fairly. People's time and knowledge have value. Pay them. Don't police how they spend it.

  • Protect the researcher. Isabella is a human absorbing trauma. Build systems that reduce unnecessary exposure, monitor wellbeing, and maintain safety.

  • Be transparent about AI. If code touches data, participants should know. Document everything.

"Current field-based research relies on commercial tools designed for clinical environments. These lack offline capability and are difficult to administer during brief field encounters. IRB & Compliance

Isabella's research involves human subjects. No research activity can begin until the IRB approves or exempts the study. Her IRB will almost certainly be IRB-HSBS (Health Sciences and Behavioral Sciences) at U-M.

Review Level Expectation

Substance-using populations are typically considered vulnerable populations under federal regulations. Expect Full Board review (monthly meetings) at minimum — not expedited. Plan for 3–9 months from submission to approval. Build this into her dissertation timeline.

Required Training

PEERRS (Program for Education and Evaluation in Responsible Research and Scholarship) — Human Subjects Research Protections course. 2–4 hours online, 85% passing score, renewed every 3 years. Everyone on the study team must complete this before working with subjects or identifiable data. If Ryan is listed as Research Staff, he must complete it too.

U-M Data Security Requirements

  • □
    Encryption at rest — AES-256 on all participant data
  • □
    Encryption in transit — TLS 1.2+
  • □
    Authentication — strong passwords, ideally U-M SSO/Shibboleth
  • □
    Access controls — role-based, study-team-only
  • □
    Identifier separation — participant IDs stored separately from data
  • □
    Audit logging — who accessed what, when
  • □
    Data export — compatible with R, SPSS, Stata, Python
  • □
    Retention controls — ability to delete/destroy per protocol timeline
  • □
    No U-M Google services for sensitive data
  • □
    Third-party vendor compliance if hosted externally

Option A

Must complete PEERRS training. Must be listed on IRB application. Gets access under same rules as other team members. More flexibility, more compliance obligations.
